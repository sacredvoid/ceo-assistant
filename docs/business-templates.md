# Business Document Templates Reference

A scannable reference for generating professional business documents. Each template includes structure, guidance, word counts, and examples ready for adaptation.

---

## 1. Lean Canvas (1-Page Business Plan)

The Lean Canvas distills a business model into a single page. Fill it out in under 20 minutes. Iterate often. The goal is clarity, not perfection.

**Fill-out order:** Problem -> Customer Segments -> Unique Value Proposition -> Solution -> Channels -> Revenue Streams -> Cost Structure -> Key Metrics -> Unfair Advantage

### Block 1: Problem (Top 3 Problems)

List the top 1-3 problems your target customers face. Be specific and observable.

- **Word count:** 15-30 words per problem
- **What to write:** Concrete pain points, not abstract concepts
- **What to avoid:** Restating your solution as the problem
- **Example:**
  - "Small restaurant owners spend 8+ hours/week on manual inventory tracking, leading to 15-20% food waste."
  - "Freelancers lose $5K+/year chasing late payments because they lack automated invoicing."
  - "Remote teams miss context in async communication, causing 3-5 redundant meetings per week."

### Block 2: Customer Segments

Who has these problems? Be narrow. "Everyone" is not a segment.

- **Word count:** 10-20 words per segment
- **What to write:** Specific demographics, roles, or company types. Identify early adopters separately.
- **What to avoid:** Overly broad segments ("SMBs," "millennials")
- **Example:**
  - **Target:** Solo restaurant owners with 1-3 locations, $500K-$2M annual revenue
  - **Early Adopter:** Tech-savvy restaurant owners already using a POS system, frustrated with manual inventory

### Block 3: Unique Value Proposition

One clear sentence explaining why you are different AND worth paying attention to.

- **Word count:** 10-20 words (single sentence or tagline)
- **Formula:** "[End result] for [target customer] without [pain/cost]"
- **What to avoid:** Feature lists, jargon, vague superlatives ("best," "revolutionary")
- **Example:**
  - "Cut food waste by 50% with AI-powered inventory that learns your kitchen's patterns."
  - "Get paid on time, every time, with invoicing that chases clients so you don't have to."

### Block 4: Solution

Map each problem to the simplest possible solution. MVP-level, not dream-state.

- **Word count:** 10-20 words per solution
- **What to write:** The minimum feature set that addresses each problem listed
- **What to avoid:** Feature bloat, technical implementation details
- **Example:**
  - Problem: Manual inventory tracking -> Solution: Auto-scan invoices with phone camera, predict reorder quantities
  - Problem: Food waste -> Solution: AI demand forecasting based on weather, events, and historical sales

### Block 5: Channels

How will you reach your customers? List the path to customers, not just marketing tactics.

- **Word count:** 5-10 words per channel
- **What to write:** Specific, testable channels ranked by expected effectiveness
- **Categories:** Free (SEO, content, social, referral), Paid (ads, sponsorships), Direct (sales, partnerships)
- **Example:**
  - Restaurant industry Facebook groups (free)
  - POS system integration marketplace (partnership)
  - Google Ads targeting "restaurant inventory software" (paid)

### Block 6: Revenue Streams

How will you make money? Be specific about pricing model and amounts.

- **Word count:** 10-20 words per stream
- **What to write:** Pricing model, price point, billing frequency
- **What to avoid:** "We'll figure out monetization later"
- **Example:**
  - Monthly SaaS subscription: $99/mo per location (Starter), $249/mo (Pro with AI forecasting)
  - Onboarding fee: $500 one-time setup and training

### Block 7: Cost Structure

List your major costs. Be honest about what it takes to run this business.

- **Word count:** 5-15 words per cost item
- **What to write:** Fixed costs (salaries, hosting, rent) and variable costs (per-customer costs, transaction fees)
- **Example:**
  - Engineering team: 3 FTEs at $150K avg = $450K/year
  - Cloud infrastructure: $2K/mo scaling to $10K/mo at 1K customers
  - Customer acquisition: Target $200 CAC

### Block 8: Key Metrics

The 3-5 numbers that tell you if the business is working. Focus on actionable metrics, not vanity metrics.

- **Word count:** 5-10 words per metric
- **What to write:** Metrics tied to growth, retention, and revenue
- **Framework:** Use pirate metrics (AARRR) - Acquisition, Activation, Retention, Revenue, Referral
- **Example:**
  - Activation: % of signups who complete first inventory scan (target: 60%)
  - Retention: Monthly churn rate (target: <5%)
  - Revenue: MRR growth rate (target: 15% MoM)
  - Referral: % of customers who refer another restaurant (target: 20%)

### Block 9: Unfair Advantage

Something that cannot be easily copied or bought. If you don't have one yet, leave it blank and build toward one.

- **Word count:** 10-25 words
- **Real unfair advantages:** Proprietary data, network effects, community, domain expertise (10+ years), existing audience, key partnerships, patents
- **NOT unfair advantages:** Features (can be copied), first mover (rarely lasting), passion, hard work
- **Example:**
  - "Proprietary dataset of 50K restaurant demand patterns across 200 cuisines, growing with every customer."
  - "Founder operated 3 restaurants for 12 years, with direct relationships to 500+ restaurant owners."

## 2. Business Model Canvas

The Business Model Canvas provides a more comprehensive view than the Lean Canvas. Better for established businesses or when presenting to stakeholders who want the full picture.

**Fill-out sequence:**
1. Start right side (value creation): Customer Segments -> Value Propositions
2. Continue right side (delivery): Channels -> Customer Relationships -> Revenue Streams
3. Then left side (infrastructure): Key Resources -> Key Activities -> Key Partnerships -> Cost Structure

### Block 1: Customer Segments

Define distinct groups of people or organizations you serve. Each segment needs its own value proposition.

- **Types:** Mass market, niche market, segmented, diversified, multi-sided platform
- **For each segment, specify:**
  - Who are they? (demographics, firmographics)
  - What job are they trying to get done?
  - What is their willingness to pay?
- **Example (multi-sided platform):**
  - **Segment A:** Freelance designers seeking project-based work ($50-150/hr range)
  - **Segment B:** Startups needing design work but can't afford full-time hires ($5K-50K project budgets)

### Block 2: Value Propositions

The bundle of products and services that create value for each customer segment.

- **Value drivers:** Newness, performance, customization, "getting the job done," design, brand/status, price, cost reduction, risk reduction, accessibility, convenience
- **Map each value prop to a customer segment**
- **Example:**
  - For freelancers: "Steady pipeline of vetted, well-scoped projects with guaranteed payment"
  - For startups: "Pre-vetted design talent matched to your project in 48 hours, not weeks"

### Block 3: Channels

How you communicate with and reach customer segments to deliver your value proposition.

- **Channel phases:** Awareness -> Evaluation -> Purchase -> Delivery -> After-sales
- **For each phase, list:**
  - Channel type (owned, partner, or a combination)
  - Specific channel
  - Cost to operate
- **Example:**
  - Awareness: Content marketing (blog, YouTube) + paid LinkedIn ads
  - Evaluation: Free consultation call + case study library
  - Purchase: Self-serve signup with credit card
  - Delivery: In-app project management dashboard
  - After-sales: Dedicated Slack channel for Pro tier

### Block 4: Customer Relationships

The type of relationship you establish with each customer segment.

- **Types:** Personal assistance, dedicated personal assistance, self-service, automated services, communities, co-creation
- **For each segment, define:**
  - Relationship type
  - Acquisition strategy
  - Retention strategy
  - Upsell strategy
- **Example:**
  - Free tier: Self-service + automated onboarding emails (7-day sequence)
  - Pro tier: Dedicated account manager, quarterly business reviews
  - Enterprise: Co-creation (custom integrations, shared product roadmap input)

### Block 5: Revenue Streams

Cash generated from each customer segment. Revenue = price x volume.

- **Pricing mechanisms:** Fixed (list price, feature-dependent, volume-dependent, segment-dependent) or Dynamic (negotiation, yield management, real-time market, auctions)
- **Revenue types:** Asset sale, usage fee, subscription, lending/renting/leasing, licensing, brokerage, advertising
- **For each stream, specify:**
  - Type, pricing model, price point, billing frequency
  - Expected contribution to total revenue (%)
- **Example:**
  - SaaS subscriptions (80% of revenue): $29/mo Starter, $99/mo Pro, $499/mo Enterprise
  - Marketplace transaction fee (15% of revenue): 10% fee on freelancer payouts
  - Training & certification (5% of revenue): $299 per course

### Block 6: Key Resources

The most important assets required to make the business model work.

- **Categories:** Physical, intellectual (patents, data, brand), human, financial
- **Example:**
  - Intellectual: Proprietary matching algorithm, curated talent database
  - Human: Engineering team (8 FTEs), sales team (4 FTEs), community manager
  - Financial: $2M seed funding, 18-month runway

### Block 7: Key Activities

The most important things the company must do to make the business model work.

- **Categories:** Production, problem solving, platform/network management
- **Example:**
  - Platform: Maintain and improve matching algorithm (weekly releases)
  - Quality: Vet and onboard new freelancers (target: 50/month)
  - Growth: Content marketing producing 8 blog posts/month
  - Sales: Enterprise outbound targeting 200 companies/quarter

### Block 8: Key Partnerships

The network of suppliers and partners that make the business model work.

- **Types:** Strategic alliances (non-competitors), coopetition (competitors), joint ventures, buyer-supplier
- **Motivations:** Optimization, reduction of risk, acquisition of resources
- **Example:**
  - Stripe (payment processing, reduced transaction fees at volume)
  - Figma/Adobe (integration partnerships, co-marketing)
  - Design bootcamps (talent pipeline, graduate placement program)

### Block 9: Cost Structure

All costs incurred to operate the business model.

- **Characteristics:** Fixed vs. variable, economies of scale, economies of scope
- **Categories:** Cost-driven (minimize costs) vs. value-driven (premium experience)
- **Example:**
  - Fixed: Salaries ($1.2M/year), office ($48K/year), SaaS tools ($24K/year)
  - Variable: Cloud hosting ($0.50/user/month), payment processing (2.9% + $0.30), customer support ($15/ticket)
  - Marketing: $50K/month (target CAC of $200, LTV:CAC ratio of 4:1)

## 3. Competitive Analysis Template

A structured approach to understanding your competitive landscape. Complete this before building your pitch deck's competition slide.

**Three-step process:**
1. Identify 3-5 competitors (direct and indirect)
2. Analyze each one deeply
3. Compare side-by-side and identify your positioning

### Per-Competitor Profile

Fill out the following for each competitor (up to 5):

```
Company Name: [Full legal/brand name]
Website: [URL]
Founded: [Year]
Funding: [Total raised, last round, investors]
Est. Revenue: [ARR or revenue range if known]
Employee Count: [Approximate headcount]

Description: [2-3 sentences: what they do, who they serve, how they position themselves]

Target Market:
- Primary segment: [Who they primarily sell to]
- Company size: [SMB / Mid-market / Enterprise]
- Geography: [Where they operate]
- Industry verticals: [Specific industries if applicable]

Key Features:
- [Feature 1]: [Brief description]
- [Feature 2]: [Brief description]
- [Feature 3]: [Brief description]
- [Feature 4]: [Brief description]
- [Feature 5]: [Brief description]

Pricing:
- Model: [Freemium / Free trial / Subscription / Usage-based / One-time]
- Entry price: [$X/mo or $X/year]
- Mid-tier: [$X/mo]
- Enterprise: [Custom / $X/mo]
- Notable: [Free tier limits, contract requirements, discounts]

Strengths:
- [Strength 1]
- [Strength 2]
- [Strength 3]

Weaknesses:
- [Weakness 1]
- [Weakness 2]
- [Weakness 3]

Customer Sentiment: [Summary from G2, Capterra, Reddit, Twitter - what do users love/hate?]

Recent Moves: [Product launches, pivots, acquisitions, layoffs in last 12 months]
```

### Comparison Matrix

After profiling each competitor, build this matrix:

```
| Feature/Attribute    | Your Product | Competitor A | Competitor B | Competitor C |
|---------------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Core feature 1      | [rating]    | [rating]    | [rating]    | [rating]    |
| Core feature 2      | [rating]    | [rating]    | [rating]    | [rating]    |
| Pricing (entry)     | $X/mo       | $X/mo       | $X/mo       | $X/mo       |
| Target segment      | [segment]   | [segment]   | [segment]   | [segment]   |
| Key differentiator  | [what]      | [what]      | [what]      | [what]      |
| Primary weakness     | [what]      | [what]      | [what]      | [what]      |
```

Use ratings: Strong / Moderate / Weak / None

### Positioning Statement (Output)

After analysis, write a positioning statement:

> "For [target customers] who [need/pain], [product name] is the [category] that [key differentiator]. Unlike [primary competitor], we [specific advantage]."

**Example:**
> "For mid-market SaaS companies who struggle with customer onboarding, FlowStart is the onboarding platform that reduces time-to-value by 60% through AI-guided workflows. Unlike WalkMe, we require zero engineering resources to set up."

## 5. Validation Interview Script (The Mom Test)

Based on Rob Fitzpatrick's "The Mom Test." The core principle: never tell people about your idea. Instead, ask about their life, their problems, and their existing behavior. Facts about the past are more reliable than opinions about the future.

### Rules

1. Talk about their life, not your idea
2. Ask about specifics in the past, not hypotheticals about the future
3. Talk less, listen more (aim for 80/20 listening to talking ratio)
4. Never ask "Would you use X?" or "Would you pay for X?" (everyone says yes to be polite)
5. Never ask leading questions ("Don't you think it would be great if...?")

### Section A: Problem Discovery (4 Questions)

These questions uncover whether the problem is real and significant.

> **Q1: "Tell me about the last time you [experienced the problem area]. Walk me through what happened."**
> - Why: Gets a concrete, specific story rather than a generalization
> - Listen for: Emotional language, workarounds they've built, time/money spent
> - Red flag: They can't recall a specific instance (the problem may not be real)

> **Q2: "What's the hardest part about [doing the thing your product helps with]?"**
> - Why: Lets them identify the pain point rather than you suggesting one
> - Listen for: Which sub-problem they mention first (that's usually the biggest)
> - Follow up: "Why is that hard?" (ask this 2-3 times to get to root cause)

> **Q3: "How are you currently dealing with [the problem]?"**
> - Why: Reveals existing solutions, workarounds, and competitive landscape
> - Listen for: Cobbled-together solutions (Excel + email + manual process = opportunity)
> - Red flag: They haven't tried to solve it at all (may not be painful enough)

> **Q4: "What have you tried that didn't work? Why did it fall short?"**
> - Why: Shows what solutions they've evaluated and rejected, and why
> - Listen for: Specific product names, specific failure modes, deal-breakers
> - Gold: "I tried X but stopped because Y" (tells you exactly what to build differently)

### Section B: Behavior & Frequency (4 Questions)

These questions validate whether the problem occurs often enough to warrant a solution.

> **Q5: "How often does this come up? Daily? Weekly? Monthly?"**
> - Why: Frequency determines willingness to pay and urgency
> - Listen for: Daily/weekly = strong signal. Monthly/yearly = weaker signal
> - Follow up: "When was the most recent time?"

> **Q6: "What does your current workflow look like from start to finish?"**
> - Why: Maps the full process so you can find the most painful step
> - Listen for: Steps that are manual, repetitive, error-prone, or involve waiting
> - Tip: Ask them to show you if possible (screen share or in-person observation)

> **Q7: "Who else is involved when this happens? Who else cares about this problem?"**
> - Why: Identifies stakeholders, decision-makers, and potential champions
> - Listen for: Other departments, managers who care about the outcome, team members affected
> - Useful for: Understanding buying committee and sales process

> **Q8: "Where does this problem rank among the things you're trying to fix right now?"**
> - Why: Even real problems may not be urgent enough to act on
> - Listen for: "Top 3" = great signal. "I'll get to it eventually" = weak signal
> - Follow up: "What's above it on the list?" (reveals competing priorities)

### Section C: Impact & Value (4 Questions)

These questions gauge willingness to pay and the value of a solution.

> **Q9: "How much time/money does this problem cost you per week/month?"**
> - Why: Quantifies the pain in dollars or hours, anchoring your pricing
> - Listen for: Specific numbers. "It costs us about $2K/month in wasted time" is gold.
> - If they can't quantify: The problem may not be painful enough to pay for

> **Q10: "If you could wave a magic wand and fix this perfectly, what would change for you?"**
> - Why: Reveals the dream outcome without biasing toward any specific solution
> - Listen for: The outcome they describe (not the solution) - that's your value proposition
> - Tip: Don't pitch your solution after they answer. Just listen and ask "What else?"

> **Q11: "Have you actively searched for a solution in the last [timeframe]? What did you look for?"**
> - Why: Active searching = strong buying signal. Specific search terms = your SEO keywords.
> - Listen for: Search terms used, sites visited, products evaluated
> - Red flag: "Not really, I've just been living with it" (low urgency)

> **Q12: "Is there a budget allocated for solving this, or would you need to make the case?"**
> - Why: Determines buying process complexity and timeline
> - Listen for: Existing budget = fast sale. No budget = longer sales cycle, need ROI proof
> - Follow up: "Who would need to approve the spend? What would convince them?"

### After the Interview

Rate the signal strength:

| Signal | Strong | Medium | Weak |
|--------|--------|--------|------|
| Problem is real | They describe specific painful incidents | They acknowledge the problem exists | They can't recall specifics |
| Problem is frequent | Daily/weekly occurrence | Monthly occurrence | Rarely happens |
| They've tried to solve it | Tried multiple solutions, still unsatisfied | Tried one thing, gave up | Haven't tried anything |
| They'd pay to solve it | Have budget allocated, actively searching | Would consider it if shown ROI | "Maybe someday" |
| They'd switch from current solution | Actively unhappy with current approach | Mildly frustrated | Current solution is "fine" |

**Strong signal = 4-5 Strong ratings. Proceed with confidence.**
**Mixed signal = 2-3 Strong ratings. Dig deeper, more interviews needed.**
**Weak signal = 0-1 Strong ratings. Pivot or find a different segment.**

## 6. Pitch Deck Template (Sequoia Format, 10-12 Slides)

Based on the Sequoia Capital pitch deck framework. Target 3-4 minutes per slide in a 30-minute meeting (leaving time for Q&A). Total slide count: 10-12. Total word count across all slides: 800-1,200 max.

### General Design Rules

- One idea per slide
- Maximum 30 words per slide (excluding data/charts)
- Use visuals over text whenever possible
- Dark text on light background for readability
- Consistent font: use 2 sizes max (header + body)
- No clip art, no stock photos of handshakes, no word clouds
- Every number should have context (vs. last period, vs. competitor, vs. industry benchmark)

---

### Slide 1: Title / Company Overview

**Purpose:** Set the stage. Establish what you do in one sentence.

**Include:**
- Company name and logo
- One-line description (what you do, for whom)
- Presenter name(s) and title(s)
- Date
- Contact info (email only)

**Do NOT include:**
- Mission statement
- Company history
- Multiple paragraphs

**Word count target:** 15-25 words

**Example:**
> **FlowStart**
> AI-powered customer onboarding that reduces time-to-value by 60%
> Jane Smith, CEO | john@flowstart.com | March 2026

---

### Slide 2: Problem

**Purpose:** Make the audience feel the pain. This is the most important slide after the ask.

**Include:**
- 2-3 specific, quantified problems
- Who experiences them (be specific)
- The cost of the status quo (time, money, or opportunity lost)
- Optional: a real customer quote

**Do NOT include:**
- Your solution (not yet)
- Vague statements ("communication is hard")
- Problems only you care about

**Word count target:** 40-60 words

**Design:** Use large numbers to make the pain tangible. "$4.2B lost annually to..." is more powerful than a paragraph.

**Example:**
> - 67% of SaaS customers never complete onboarding
> - Average time-to-value: 47 days (customers churn at 30)
> - Companies lose $1.2M/year in revenue from failed onboarding
> - "We spent 6 months building the product, then lost users in the first week." - VP Product, Series B SaaS

---

### Slide 3: Solution

**Purpose:** Show how you solve the problems on the previous slide. Keep it simple.

**Include:**
- Your solution in 1-2 sentences
- 3 key capabilities (mapped to the 3 problems)
- A visual (screenshot, diagram, or short demo GIF)

**Do NOT include:**
- Technical architecture
- Feature lists longer than 3 items
- Roadmap items not yet built

**Word count target:** 30-50 words

**Design:** Screenshot or product visual should take up 60%+ of the slide.

---

### Slide 4: Market Size (TAM / SAM / SOM)

**Purpose:** Prove this is a big enough opportunity to warrant venture investment.

**Include:**
- **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** The total global market if you had 100% share
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):** The segment you can realistically reach with your current business model
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** What you can capture in 3-5 years
- Source for each number (analyst reports, bottom-up calculation)
- Bottom-up calculation preferred (# of customers x annual contract value)

**Do NOT include:**
- TAM-only without SAM/SOM (looks like you haven't done the work)
- Top-down only ("the market is $50B" with no narrowing)
- Markets you clearly can't address

**Word count target:** 30-50 words + 3 numbers

**Design:** Concentric circles or nested boxes. TAM (outermost) -> SAM -> SOM (innermost). Label each with dollar amount.

**Example (bottom-up):**
> - 150K mid-market SaaS companies globally (TAM: $12B)
> - 40K in North America with 500+ customers (SAM: $3.2B)
> - Target 2% in 5 years: 800 companies at $40K ACV (SOM: $32M ARR)

---

### Slide 5: Product Demo / Screenshots

**Purpose:** Show the product in action. This is where you build excitement.

**Include:**
- 2-4 screenshots of key workflows
- Brief captions explaining what the user sees
- Ideally: a live demo or embedded video (60-90 seconds)

**Do NOT include:**
- Settings pages, admin panels, or login screens
- Mockups if you have a real product
- Every feature - show the "aha moment" only

**Word count target:** 20-30 words (let the visuals do the work)

**Design:** Full-bleed screenshots with minimal UI chrome. Annotate with arrows or highlights if needed.

---

### Slide 6: Business Model

**Purpose:** Explain how you make money. Be specific.

**Include:**
- Pricing model (subscription, usage-based, marketplace fee, etc.)
- Price points by tier
- Average contract value (ACV) or average revenue per user (ARPU)
- Unit economics if available: CAC, LTV, LTV:CAC ratio, payback period

**Do NOT include:**
- "We'll figure out monetization later"
- Pricing you haven't tested
- Complex multi-variable pricing that confuses the audience

**Word count target:** 40-60 words

**Example:**
> - SaaS subscription: $2K/mo (Growth), $5K/mo (Scale), $12K/mo (Enterprise)
> - Current ACV: $42K | Target ACV: $60K by Q4
> - CAC: $8K (blended) | LTV: $126K | LTV:CAC: 15.8x
> - Payback period: 4 months

---

### Slide 7: Traction / Metrics

**Purpose:** Prove this isn't just an idea. Show real progress.

**Include:**
- Revenue (MRR/ARR) with growth rate
- Customer count and growth
- Key engagement metrics (DAU/MAU, retention, NPS)
- Notable logos (if B2B)
- Growth chart showing trajectory (up and to the right)

**Do NOT include:**
- Vanity metrics (downloads, page views, registered users who never activated)
- Metrics without timeframes
- Flat or declining charts (address slowdowns verbally, don't chart them)

**Word count target:** 30-50 words + one chart

**Design:** One clean growth chart dominates the slide. Key metrics as large numbers with labels below/beside the chart.

**Example:**
> - $1.2M ARR (3x YoY growth)
> - 47 paying customers (up from 12 last year)
> - 94% gross retention, 118% net retention
> - NPS: 72
> - Logos: [Stripe, Notion, Figma, Vercel]

---

### Slide 8: Competition

**Purpose:** Show you understand the landscape and have a defensible position.

**Include:**
- 2x2 matrix positioning you in the upper-right quadrant
- Choose axes that highlight your advantage (not generic "features vs. price")
- Brief explanation of why your positioning is defensible
- Acknowledge competitors exist (never say "we have no competitors")

**Do NOT include:**
- Checkmark comparison tables where you win every row (nobody believes this)
- More than 6 competitors on one chart
- Competitors the audience has never heard of

**Word count target:** 25-40 words + the matrix visual

## 8. SWOT Analysis Template

A strategic planning tool for evaluating internal strengths/weaknesses and external opportunities/threats. Best used during annual planning, before major strategic decisions, or when entering new markets.

### How to Use

1. Gather 3-5 stakeholders (founders, department leads)
2. Brainstorm each quadrant independently (10 min each)
3. Prioritize: pick top 3 per quadrant
4. Create action items: leverage strengths, address weaknesses, pursue opportunities, mitigate threats

### Strengths (Internal, Positive)

Things your company does well or resources you have that competitors don't.

**Prompt questions:**
- What do we do better than anyone else in our market?
- What unique resources or capabilities do we have?
- What do our customers say they love about us?
- What's our strongest competitive advantage right now?
- Where is our team exceptionally strong?
- What processes or systems give us an edge?

**Template:**
```
STRENGTHS:
1. [Strength]: [Evidence/quantification]
   Impact: [How this translates to business value]

2. [Strength]: [Evidence/quantification]
   Impact: [How this translates to business value]

3. [Strength]: [Evidence/quantification]
   Impact: [How this translates to business value]
```

**Example:**
```
1. Industry-leading NPS of 72 (industry avg: 31)
   Impact: 40% of new customers come from referrals, reducing CAC by 60%

2. Proprietary dataset of 2M+ onboarding flows across 500 companies
   Impact: ML models improve recommendations with each new customer, creating a data moat

3. Founding team has 45 combined years in customer success
   Impact: Deep domain expertise accelerates product-market fit and enterprise credibility
```

### Weaknesses (Internal, Negative)

Areas where you fall short or lack resources compared to competitors.

**Prompt questions:**
- Where do we consistently lose deals? Why?
- What do customers complain about most?
- Where are we understaffed or under-skilled?
- What processes are broken or manual?
- What do competitors do better than us?
- Where is our technical debt highest?

**Template:**
```
WEAKNESSES:
1. [Weakness]: [Evidence/quantification]
   Mitigation plan: [What we're doing about it]
   Timeline: [When we expect improvement]

2. [Weakness]: [Evidence/quantification]
   Mitigation plan: [What we're doing about it]
   Timeline: [When we expect improvement]

3. [Weakness]: [Evidence/quantification]
   Mitigation plan: [What we're doing about it]
   Timeline: [When we expect improvement]
```

### Opportunities (External, Positive)

External trends, market changes, or gaps you can exploit.

**Prompt questions:**
- What market trends favor our business?
- Are there underserved segments we could enter?
- What regulatory or technology changes create openings?
- Are competitors leaving gaps we can fill?
- Are there partnership opportunities we haven't pursued?
- Is there adjacent market demand for our capabilities?

**Template:**
```
OPPORTUNITIES:
1. [Opportunity]: [Market evidence/data]
   Potential impact: [Revenue or growth estimate]
   Action required: [What we need to do to capture it]
   Urgency: [High/Medium/Low - is this time-sensitive?]

2. [Opportunity]: [Market evidence/data]
   Potential impact: [Revenue or growth estimate]
   Action required: [What we need to do to capture it]
   Urgency: [High/Medium/Low]

3. [Opportunity]: [Market evidence/data]
   Potential impact: [Revenue or growth estimate]
   Action required: [What we need to do to capture it]
   Urgency: [High/Medium/Low]
```

### Threats (External, Negative)

External factors that could harm your business.

**Prompt questions:**
- Which competitors are growing fastest? What are they doing?
- What economic conditions could impact our customers' budgets?
- Are there regulatory changes that could affect us?
- Is new technology making our approach obsolete?
- Are customer needs shifting away from our solution?
- Could a large platform build our core feature?

**Template:**
```
THREATS:
1. [Threat]: [Evidence/likelihood]
   Potential impact: [Severity: High/Medium/Low]
   Contingency plan: [How we'd respond]
   Early warning signs: [What to watch for]

2. [Threat]: [Evidence/likelihood]
   Potential impact: [Severity: High/Medium/Low]
   Contingency plan: [How we'd respond]
   Early warning signs: [What to watch for]

3. [Threat]: [Evidence/likelihood]
   Potential impact: [Severity: High/Medium/Low]
   Contingency plan: [How we'd respond]
   Early warning signs: [What to watch for]
```

### SWOT Action Matrix

After completing all four quadrants, create an action matrix:

```
| Strategy Type | Combines | Action |
|--------------|---------|--------|
| SO (Maxi-Maxi) | Strengths + Opportunities | Use strengths to capitalize on opportunities |
| WO (Mini-Maxi) | Weaknesses + Opportunities | Overcome weaknesses to pursue opportunities |
| ST (Maxi-Mini) | Strengths + Threats | Use strengths to mitigate threats |
| WT (Mini-Mini) | Weaknesses + Threats | Minimize weaknesses and avoid threats |
```

A checklist to review before any investor pitch. Each mistake includes why it's a problem and how to fix it.

### 1. Starting with the Solution, Not the Problem

**The mistake:** Leading with "We built X" instead of "Here's why X needs to exist."
**Why it fails:** Investors need to believe the problem is worth solving before they care about your solution.
**Fix:** Spend slide 2 making the audience feel the pain. Use data and customer quotes. Only then introduce your solution.

### 2. Unrealistic TAM

**The mistake:** Claiming a $50B TAM by citing the broadest possible market definition.
**Why it fails:** Investors immediately discount founders who can't distinguish TAM from SAM from SOM.
**Fix:** Use bottom-up calculation. Number of target customers x realistic ACV = credible market size.

### 3. No Clear Business Model

**The mistake:** "We'll monetize once we have scale" or hand-waving about future revenue.
**Why it fails:** No business model = no business. Even pre-revenue companies need a pricing hypothesis.
**Fix:** Show specific pricing tiers, tested or benchmarked. Include unit economics even if estimated.

### 4. Ignoring Competition

**The mistake:** "We have no competitors" or listing only companies in a different category.
**Why it fails:** Every problem has an existing solution, even if it's Excel and email. Claiming no competitors signals either naivety or a market that doesn't exist.
**Fix:** Acknowledge direct and indirect competitors. Show your positioning on a 2x2 matrix. Explain why you win.

### 5. Too Much Text on Slides

**The mistake:** Slides with 100+ words that the presenter reads aloud.
**Why it fails:** Audiences read faster than you talk. They stop listening and start reading.
**Fix:** Maximum 30 words per slide. Use visuals, charts, and images. Let your narration add the detail.

### 6. Vague Traction Claims

**The mistake:** "Strong traction" or "growing fast" without specific numbers.
**Why it fails:** Investors need numbers to evaluate. Vague claims suggest the numbers aren't good.
**Fix:** Show exact figures with timeframes: "$180K MRR, 3x YoY" or "47 customers, 94% retention."

### 7. Weak Team Slide

**The mistake:** Listing names and generic titles without relevant credentials.
**Why it fails:** Investors bet on teams. They need to see why THIS team wins THIS market.
**Fix:** Highlight domain expertise, relevant company experience, and specific achievements. "Built X at Y that did Z."

### 8. Unrealistic Financial Projections

**The mistake:** Year 3 revenue of $100M with no credible path from current numbers.
**Why it fails:** It signals the founder doesn't understand their own growth levers.
**Fix:** Show internally consistent assumptions. If you project 500 customers at $40K ACV, explain the sales capacity, pipeline, and conversion rates that get you there.

### 9. Wrong Funding Ask

**The mistake:** Asking for too much ("$20M seed") or too little ("$200K to build everything") for the stage.
**Why it fails:** Shows misunderstanding of what the funding market supports and what the business needs.
**Fix:** Research typical round sizes for your stage and geography. Tie the ask to specific milestones: "We're raising $4M to reach $6M ARR and Series B readiness in 18 months."

### 10. No Clear Ask

**The mistake:** Ending without specifying how much you're raising, what round it is, or what terms you're seeking.
**Why it fails:** Investors can't engage if they don't know what you want.
**Fix:** Dedicated "The Ask" slide with exact amount, use of funds, and milestones.

### 11. Burying the Moat

**The mistake:** Failing to clearly articulate what makes you defensible.
**Why it fails:** If competitors can replicate your product in 6 months, investors won't fund you.
**Fix:** Call out your unfair advantage explicitly: proprietary data, network effects, regulatory moat, technical depth, or unique distribution.

### 12. Deck is Too Long

**The mistake:** 30+ slides or a 60-minute presentation.
**Why it fails:** Attention drops after 15-20 minutes. Long decks signal inability to prioritize.
**Fix:** 10-12 slides, 20-minute pitch, 10 minutes for Q&A. Move supporting detail to an appendix.

### 13. No Customer Validation

**The mistake:** Presenting a product built entirely on founder assumptions.
**Why it fails:** Investors want evidence that customers want this, not just that you want to build it.
**Fix:** Include customer quotes, pilot results, waitlist numbers, or LOIs. Even 5 strong interviews are better than none.

### 14. Features Over Benefits

**The mistake:** "Our product has AI-powered analytics, real-time dashboards, and API integrations."
**Why it fails:** Customers don't buy features, they buy outcomes. Investors know this.
**Fix:** Translate every feature into a benefit: "Our AI-powered analytics reduce decision-making time from 2 hours to 5 minutes."

### 15. Getting Defensive Under Questions

**The mistake:** Arguing with investor questions or dismissing concerns.
**Why it fails:** Investors are stress-testing you. Defensiveness signals poor coachability.
**Fix:** Acknowledge the concern, share your thinking, and if you don't know, say "That's a great question, we're still figuring that out. Here's our current hypothesis."

### 16. No Product Demo

**The mistake:** All talk, no show. Especially for seed and Series A.
**Why it fails:** A working demo is worth a thousand slides. It builds credibility instantly.
**Fix:** Include a 60-90 second live demo or embedded video. Show the "aha moment" only. Skip the admin panel.

### 17. Ignoring Known Weaknesses

**The mistake:** Hoping investors won't notice your gaps.
**Why it fails:** They always notice. Not addressing weaknesses looks like you're hiding something.
**Fix:** Proactively acknowledge 1-2 key risks and explain your mitigation plan.

### 18. Generic Value Proposition

**The mistake:** "We're the best/fastest/easiest solution for [broad category]."
**Why it fails:** It's forgettable and interchangeable with every competitor.
**Fix:** Be specific: "We reduce SaaS onboarding time by 60% through AI-guided workflows, requiring zero engineering effort." Specific > clever.

### 19. Poor Slide Design

**The mistake:** Inconsistent fonts, crowded layouts, low-res images, walls of bullet points.
**Why it fails:** Design quality signals attention to detail. A messy deck suggests a messy operation.
**Fix:** Use a clean template (Pitch, Canva, or hire a designer for $500). Consistent fonts, colors, and spacing. White space is your friend.

### 20. No Vision Beyond the Raise

**The mistake:** Ending the pitch without painting a picture of the future.
**Why it fails:** Investors want to fund a journey, not a product. They need to see where this goes.
**Fix:** Close with a compelling vision slide. Where does this company go in 5-10 years? What does the world look like when you win?

## 12. Comparison Decision Frameworks

Quick-reference frameworks for common strategic decisions. For each comparison: when to choose each option, key decision criteria, and a decision prompt.

---

### Bootstrap vs. VC Funding

**Choose Bootstrap if:**
- You want to maintain full ownership and control
- Profitability is more important than hypergrowth
- You're building a B2B SaaS with strong unit economics
- You can reach $1M+ ARR through organic growth
- Your market doesn't have winner-take-all dynamics
- You have personal savings or revenue to fund operations
- You're building a "lifestyle" business (this is not a pejorative - many $5-20M ARR bootstrapped companies exist)

**Choose VC if:**
- You're in a winner-take-all or winner-take-most market
- Speed matters more than efficiency (land grab)
- You need significant capital before revenue (hardware, marketplace, deep tech)
- You're targeting a $100M+ exit
- You need credibility/network that comes with brand-name investors
- The market opportunity is time-sensitive (regulatory window, platform shift)

**Key decision criteria:**
| Factor | Bootstrap | VC |
|--------|-----------|-----|
| Ownership at exit | 100% | 15-30% (typical founder at Series B) |
| Growth expectation | 20-50% YoY | 100-300% YoY |
| Time to profitability | 6-18 months | 3-7 years |
| Hiring speed | 1-2 per quarter | 5-10 per quarter |
| Exit pressure | None (optional) | Expected within 7-10 years |
| Revenue at $10M ARR (you keep) | $10M | $1.5-3M (dilution) |

**Decision prompt:**
> "Can I reach $1M ARR in 18 months without external capital? If yes, bootstrap. If no, why not? If the answer is 'the market requires speed,' VC may be right. If the answer is 'I need to build for 2 years before revenue,' reconsider the business model first."

---

### Angel vs. VC

**Choose Angel Investment if:**
- Raising $25K-$500K
- Pre-seed or seed stage
- You want mentorship and industry connections
- Valuation discussions are simpler (SAFEs or convertible notes)
- You need fast decisions (angels can decide in 1-2 meetings)
- Strategic angels can open doors (industry executives, potential customers)

**Choose VC if:**
- Raising $500K+ (seed) or $2M+ (Series A)
- You need institutional credibility for enterprise sales
- You want a board member who's done this before
- You're ready for structured governance and reporting
- You need follow-on funding capability

**Key differences:**
| Factor | Angel | VC |
|--------|-------|-----|
| Check size | $25K-$500K | $500K-$50M+ |
| Decision speed | 1-4 weeks | 2-6 months |
| Due diligence | Light (personal conviction) | Heavy (data room, references) |
| Board involvement | Advisory only | Board seat typically |
| Follow-on | Discretionary (may not have reserves) | Reserves for follow-on rounds |
| Instrument | SAFE or convertible note | Priced round (preferred equity) |

---

### Lean Canvas vs. Traditional Business Plan

**Choose Lean Canvas if:**
- Building a new SaaS, marketplace, or tech product
- Business model is unproven and will evolve
- You need to iterate quickly (weekly pivots)
- Your audience is startup-savvy (accelerators, angels, early VCs)
- Time is scarce (fill out in 20 minutes, not 20 days)

**Choose Traditional Business Plan if:**
- Applying for a bank loan or SBA loan
- Opening a franchise
- Need for equipment financing or real estate investment
- Regulated industry requiring detailed operational plans
- Your audience expects it (certain grant applications, government programs, corporate ventures)

---

### Product-Led Growth (PLG) vs. Sales-Led Growth (SLG)

**Choose PLG if:**
- Price point < $100/month
- Product can be self-served (signup, onboard, pay without talking to anyone)
- End users are the buyers (bottom-up adoption)
- Product has a natural viral loop or network effect
- Short time-to-value (user sees benefit in minutes, not months)
- Large target market (thousands of potential customers)

**Choose SLG if:**
- Price point > $1K/month
- Buying process involves multiple stakeholders (procurement, legal, IT)
- Product requires customization, integration, or training
- Customers need white-glove onboarding
- Small number of high-value accounts (hundreds, not thousands)
- Regulatory/compliance requirements need human guidance

**Hybrid approach:**
Many successful companies use PLG for acquisition + SLG for expansion. Start self-serve, then layer in sales for upsell to enterprise. Examples: Slack, Figma, Notion, Datadog.

| Factor | PLG | SLG |
|--------|-----|------|
| CAC | $50-500 | $5K-50K |
| Sales cycle | Minutes to days | Weeks to months |
| Team needed | Product + growth eng | AEs + SDRs + SEs |
| Onboarding | Self-serve / in-app | Managed / white-glove |
| Key metric | Activation rate | Pipeline coverage |
| Scales via | Product improvements | Headcount |

---

### Freemium vs. Free Trial

**Choose Freemium if:**
- Product has a viral/network component (more users = more value)
- Free tier serves as a marketing channel (user invites colleagues)
- Cost to serve free users is near-zero
- Conversion from free to paid is driven by usage limits, not time
- You're building a broad user base for platform effects

**Choose Free Trial if:**
- Product value is immediately apparent but time-limited exploration works
- Cost to serve each user is meaningful
- You want to create urgency to convert
- Product is complex and needs a guided evaluation period
- Target buyer expects a trial (B2B enterprise norms)

| Factor | Freemium | Free Trial |
|--------|---------|-----------|
| Conversion rate | 2-5% typical | 10-25% typical |
| User base | Large (millions possible) | Smaller (thousands) |
| Urgency to convert | Low (no deadline) | High (trial expires) |
| Cost to support | Must be minimal | Acceptable (time-limited) |
| Revenue timing | Delayed | Faster |
| Examples | Slack, Spotify, Figma | Salesforce, HubSpot, Netflix |

**Decision prompt:**
> "Would a user who never pays still generate value for me (referrals, data, content, network effects)? If yes, freemium. If no, free trial."

---

### No-Code MVP vs. Custom Development

**Choose No-Code if:**
- Validating an idea before investing in custom development
- Budget is under $10K
- MVP needs to be live in 2-4 weeks
- Core functionality maps to existing no-code capabilities (forms, workflows, databases, landing pages)
- You don't have a technical co-founder
- 80% of your vision can be achieved with existing tools

**Choose Custom Development if:**
- Product requires unique technical capabilities (ML, real-time processing, hardware integration)
- Performance and scale requirements exceed no-code limits
- You need full control over UX and branding
- Idea is validated and you're building for scale
- You have engineering resources (co-founder or team)
- No-code tools create vendor lock-in that's unacceptable

**Recommended no-code stack (2025-2026):**
| Need | Tool | Cost |
|------|------|------|
| Web app (database-backed) | Bubble, Softr, Glide | $30-100/mo |
| Automations | Make (Integromat), Zapier, n8n | $20-100/mo |
| Landing pages | Framer, Webflow, Carrd | $0-30/mo |
| Forms & surveys | Tally, Typeform | $0-50/mo |
| Payments | Stripe (direct) | 2.9% + $0.30 |
| Auth | Clerk, Auth0 | Free-$25/mo |
| Database | Airtable, Supabase | $0-25/mo |

**Decision prompt:**
> "Can I validate this idea with 100 paying customers using no-code tools? If yes, start there. Build custom only after validation."

---

### Solo Founder vs. Co-Founder

**Go Solo if:**
- Building a lifestyle or bootstrapped business
- You have all critical skills (or can outsource affordably)
- You want full decision-making authority
- Your market doesn't require venture capital
- You work best independently
- The business is an extension of your existing expertise/audience

**Find a Co-Founder if:**
- Pursuing venture-backed growth (VCs strongly prefer 2+ founders)
- You have significant skill gaps (e.g., you're non-technical building a tech product)
- You need emotional resilience support (startups are lonely)
- The workload genuinely requires two full-time leaders
- Complementary skills would accelerate product-market fit

**Co-founder fit criteria:**
1. **Complementary skills:** One builds, one sells (or one does product, one does growth)
2. **Shared values:** Alignment on vision, work ethic, risk tolerance, and exit goals
3. **Stress tested:** Work on a project together for 3-6 months before making it official
4. **Equity conversation:** Have the uncomfortable equity/vesting conversation early. Standard: 4-year vest, 1-year cliff.
5. **Operating agreement:** Define decision-making authority, dispute resolution, and departure terms in writing

**Red flags in potential co-founders:**
- Won't commit full-time (or won't set a date to go full-time)
- Only wants to be "idea person" without execution responsibilities
- Can't clearly articulate what they'll personally build/do in the first 90 days
- Wants equal equity without equal commitment
- Has never shipped anything independently

## 13. Investor Update Email Template

Monthly email update for investors (distinct from the slide-based board deck).

### Subject Line
`[Company Name] - [Month] [Year] Update`

### Template

```
Hi [investors],

[1-2 sentence summary. Set the tone honestly - was this a good month or tough month?]

HIGHLIGHTS
- [Specific win with metric, e.g., "Crossed $20K MRR, up 18% MoM"]
- [Win with metric]
- [Win with metric]

KEY METRICS
          This Month    Last Month    Change
MRR       $X            $X            +X%
Customers X             X             +X
Churn     X%            X%            [direction]
CAC       $X            $X            [direction]
Runway    X months      X months      [direction]

CHALLENGES
- [Challenge + what you're doing about it]
- [Challenge + plan to address]

PRODUCT
- [What shipped this month]
- [What's shipping next month]

CASH
- Balance: $X
- Monthly burn: $X
- Runway: X months

ASKS (be specific)
- [e.g., "Looking for intro to VP Sales candidates with B2B SaaS experience"]
- [e.g., "Need feedback on our new pricing page - link"]

Best,
[Name]
```

### Best Practices
- Send mid-week (Wed/Thu), same day each month
- Keep under 800 words, scannable in 5 minutes
- Same metrics every month so investors can spot trends
- Never bury bad news - address it directly with a plan
- Always include specific asks (not "let me know if you can help")
- Companies sending monthly updates see 40% higher follow-on funding rates

The core strategic document in the Entrepreneurial Operating System. 8 questions that define your company.

### Template

**1. Core Values** (3-7 values)
- [ ] [Value 1: description]
- [ ] [Value 2: description]

**3. 10-Year Target**
- [Single big audacious goal. Revenue target, market position, or impact metric]

**4. Marketing Strategy**

- 3 Uniques: [What 3 things differentiate you from every competitor?]

- Guarantee: [What promise do you make to customers?]

**5. 3-Year Picture**
- Revenue: $[X]

**6. 1-Year Plan**
- Revenue goal: $[X]
- Profit goal: $[X]

- [Rock 1: Owner - specific deliverable - due date]

(3-7 per company, 3-7 per person)

- [Issue 3]

### Usage
- Complete once, review quarterly
- Rocks reset every 90 days
- Issues list is ongoing - use IDS (Identify, Discuss, Solve) in weekly L10 meetings
- Share with entire company for alignment

## 15. Startup Business Plan Template

Full business plan structure for bank loans, accelerator applications, or detailed planning.

### Sections (15-30 pages total)

**1. Executive Summary** (1-2 pages, written last)
- Company mission (1 sentence)
- Product/service description
- Target market and customer
- Business model and revenue
- Traction to date
- Funding ask and use of funds
- Key team members

**2. Company Description** (1 page)
- Legal structure (C-Corp, LLC, etc.)
- Founding date and location
- Company history and milestones
- Mission, vision, values

**3. Market Analysis** (3-5 pages)
- Industry overview and trends
- TAM/SAM/SOM with methodology
- Target customer profile
- Market growth drivers
- Regulatory environment

**4. Competitive Analysis** (2-3 pages)
- Direct competitors (3-5)
- Indirect competitors
- Competitive advantages
- Positioning strategy
- Barriers to entry

**5. Products & Services** (2-3 pages)
- Core offering description
- Key features and benefits
- Technology/IP
- Development roadmap
- Pricing strategy

**6. Marketing & Sales Strategy** (2-4 pages)
- Go-to-market approach
- Customer acquisition channels
- Sales process
- Partnerships
- Brand strategy

**7. Operations** (1-2 pages)
- Team structure and key roles
- Technology infrastructure
- Key processes
- Vendors and partners

**8. Financial Plan** (3-5 pages)
- Revenue model assumptions
- 3-year P&L projection
- Cash flow forecast
- Break-even analysis
- Funding requirements and use of funds

**9. Appendices**
- Detailed financial models
- Customer testimonials/LOIs
- Patent/IP documentation
- Team resumes
