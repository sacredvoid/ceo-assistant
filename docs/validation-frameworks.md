## 1. Master Validation Framework (5-Step)

Execute these steps sequentially. Each step builds on the previous one. Stop and pivot if red flags emerge at any stage.

| Step | Activity | Timeline | Minimum Threshold | Key Output |
|------|----------|----------|-------------------|------------|
| 1 | Market Research & Sizing (TAM/SAM/SOM) | Week 1-2 | TAM >= $10M | Market size estimate, competitor map, growth trends |
| 2 | Customer Discovery Interviews | Week 1-3 | 30-50 interviews minimum | Pain point validation, willingness-to-pay data, behavioral patterns |
| 3 | Landing Page Testing | Week 3-4 | 500+ unique visitors | Conversion rate, email signups, messaging resonance |
| 4 | Pre-sales / Pilot Programs | Week 4-5 | 3-5 signed LOIs or deposits | Revenue signals, pricing validation, commitment level |
| 5 | MVP Prototype Testing | Week 5+ | 10-20 active users over 2-4 weeks | Retention, engagement, NPS, feature usage data |

### Step 1: Market Research & Sizing

- **TAM (Total Addressable Market):** Total revenue opportunity if 100% market share. Use top-down (industry reports) and bottom-up (# of customers x avg revenue per customer) approaches.
- **SAM (Serviceable Addressable Market):** Segment of TAM you can realistically reach with your product and distribution. Typically 10-30% of TAM.
- **SOM (Serviceable Obtainable Market):** Realistic capture in first 2-3 years. Typically 1-5% of SAM for a startup.

Sources: Statista, IBISWorld, Grand View Research, CB Insights, Crunchbase, public company filings (10-K), census data, trade associations.

### Step 2: Customer Discovery Interviews

See [Section 3 (Mom Test)](#3-the-mom-test-interview-framework) and [Section 12 (Customer Discovery)](#12-customer-discovery) for detailed methodology.

### Step 3: Landing Page Testing

- Build a simple landing page describing the value proposition (not the product).
- Drive 500+ unique visitors via targeted ads ($200-500 budget).
- Measure: email signup rate, CTA click rate, scroll depth, time on page.
- Target: 2-5%+ email conversion rate.

### Step 4: Pre-sales / Pilot Programs

- Offer early access pricing, LOIs, or refundable deposits.
- Target: 3-5 signed commitments before building.
- Strongest signal: people pay before the product exists.

### Step 5: MVP Prototype Testing

- Build the minimum version that delivers core value.
- Recruit 10-20 users from interview pool or landing page signups.
- Run for 2-4 weeks minimum.
- Track: daily/weekly active usage, retention at day 1/7/14/30, NPS, feature requests.

## 2. Validation Signals

### Green Flags (Proceed)

| Signal | Threshold | Why It Matters |
|--------|-----------|----------------|
| Pain consistency | 15+ customers describe identical pain unprompted | Real problem, not imagined |
| Market size | TAM >= $10M | Room to build a real business |
| Landing page conversion | 2-5%+ email signup rate | Messaging resonates with strangers |
| Unit economics | LTV/CAC >= 3:1 | Sustainable growth possible |
| Pull signal | People ask "when can I buy?" or "can I pay now?" | Demand exists without pushing |
| Willingness to pay | Prospects name a price without anchoring | Value is clear and quantifiable |
| Existing budget | Customers already spend money on workarounds | Money is flowing, you just redirect it |
| Retention signal | Users return without prompting | Core loop works |
| Referral behavior | Users share with others unprompted | Organic growth potential |

### Red Flags (Pause or Pivot)

| Signal | Threshold | What It Means |
|--------|-----------|---------------|
| No pain cluster | Can't find 15+ people with the same pain | Problem may not exist at scale |
| Small market | TAM under $10M | Ceiling is too low for venture or meaningful business |
| Low conversion | <1% landing page conversion | Messaging or problem is off |
| No existing solutions | Nobody spends money or time on workarounds | Problem isn't painful enough to solve |
| Bad unit economics | CAC > LTV | Business loses money on every customer |
| Politeness problem | People say "cool idea" but won't commit time or money | Social desirability bias, not real demand |
| "Nice to have" language | Prospects say "I'd probably use it" or "sounds interesting" | Not a must-have, won't pay |
| Feature creep requests | Every prospect wants something completely different | No coherent problem to solve |

## 3. The Mom Test Interview Framework

Based on Rob Fitzpatrick's "The Mom Test." The core rule: talk about their life, not your idea. Extract facts about past behavior, not opinions about future behavior.

### Problem Discovery Questions

Ask these to understand whether a real problem exists and how people experience it.

- "Walk me through the last time you dealt with [problem area]."
- "What's the hardest part about [process/task]?"
- "How often does this come up? Daily, weekly, monthly?"
- "What have you already tried to solve this?"
- "Why didn't those solutions work?"
- "What does a good day vs bad day look like for [activity]?"
- "If you could wave a magic wand and fix one thing about [area], what would it be?"

### Behavior & Frequency Questions

Ask these to gauge how real and urgent the problem is.

- "How much time do you spend on this per week?"
- "How much money do you spend on current solutions?"
- "When was the last time this happened?"
- "What did you do about it?"
- "Have you actively searched for a better solution? Where?"
- "What would you do if your current tool/process disappeared tomorrow?"

### Impact & Value Questions

Ask these to understand willingness to pay and decision-making.

- "What does this problem cost you in revenue, time, or stress?"
- "Who else is affected by this problem in your organization?"
- "Who would need to approve a purchase for something like this?"
- "What's your budget for tools in this area?"
- "What would a solution need to do for you to switch from what you use now?"

### Questions You Must NEVER Ask

| Bad Question Type | Example | Why It Fails |
|-------------------|---------|--------------|
| Leading | "Don't you think X would be useful?" | Plants the answer |
| Yes/No | "Would you use a product that does X?" | Everyone says yes to be polite |
| Hypothetical | "If we built X, would you pay $Y?" | Imagined behavior != real behavior |
| Opinion-seeking | "What do you think of this idea?" | Opinions are worthless, behavior is everything |
| Future-predicting | "How much would you pay for this?" | People cannot predict their own purchasing behavior |
| Pitching disguised as research | "Let me tell you about what we're building..." | You learn nothing |

### Interview Execution Rules

1. **Listen 80%, talk 20%.** If you're talking more than the interviewee, you're doing it wrong.
2. **Never mention your idea** until the very end (if at all).
3. **Push past compliments.** When they say "that's cool," ask "would you pay for it today?"
4. **Get specifics.** "Sometimes" means nothing. "Three times last week" means something.
5. **Ask for introductions.** "Who else deals with this?" snowballs your pipeline.
6. **Take notes immediately** after each interview, not during. Be present.
7. **Batch interviews.** Do 5-10 in a week, then synthesize patterns before the next batch.

## 4. Validation Hierarchy

Evidence ranked from strongest to weakest. Always try to collect evidence from the top of this list. Lower levels are starting points, not destinations.

| Rank | Evidence Type | Description | Example | Weight |
|------|--------------|-------------|---------|--------|
| 1 | **Transactions** | Actual payment made | Customer pays $500 for annual license | 5x |
| 2 | **Commitments** | Signed contract, deposit, or LOI | Customer signs LOI for pilot program | 4x |
| 3 | **Revealed Behavior** | Time or money spent on alternatives | Customer spends 10 hrs/week on manual spreadsheets | 3x |
| 4 | **Stated Behavior** | Interview responses about past actions | "I searched for solutions last month" | 2x |
| 5 | **Opinions** | Survey responses, social media reactions | 500 people upvote your concept on Reddit | 1x |

### How to Use This Hierarchy

- **Never stop at Level 5.** Opinions alone are not validation.
- **Level 3 is the minimum** to justify building anything.
- **Level 1-2 before building** is the gold standard. If people won't commit before you build, they probably won't commit after.
- **Each level up is roughly 2-5x more predictive** of actual success than the level below it.

## 5. Product Validation vs Market Validation

These are different activities with different questions, methods, and timing. Do not conflate them.

| Dimension | Market Validation | Product Validation |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Core Question** | "Should we build this?" | "Did we build it right?" |
| **Timing** | Before building (pre-product) | During and after building |
| **Focus** | Problem existence, market size, willingness to pay | Usability, feature fit, retention, satisfaction |
| **Methods** | Customer interviews, landing pages, pre-sales, market sizing | Beta testing, A/B tests, analytics, NPS surveys |
| **Success Metrics** | TAM/SAM/SOM, # of people with pain, conversion rate, LOIs | DAU/MAU, retention curves, NPS, feature adoption, churn |
| **Failure Mode** | Building something nobody wants | Building the right thing poorly |
| **Cost of Skipping** | Total project failure (42% of startups fail from no market need) | Slow growth, high churn, costly rework |

### Sequencing

```
Market Validation (Weeks 1-5)
    |
    v
[GO / NO-GO decision]
    |
    v
Product Validation (Ongoing after launch)
```

Always validate the market before validating the product. The most common startup mistake is skipping directly to product validation.

## 6. Viability Scoring

**Use the 15-criteria weighted rubric in [validation-scoring-rubric.md](validation-scoring-rubric.md) for all scoring.** It provides explicit 1-10 guidelines per criterion with weights totaling 100%.

The 15 criteria map to these high-level domains:

| Domain | Rubric Criteria | Combined Weight |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| Market Timing | #1 Market Timing | 12% |
| Market Size & Growth | #2 TAM, #3 Growth Rate | 18% |
| Problem & Demand | #4 Problem Severity, #5 Willingness to Pay | 16% |
| Competition | #6 Competition, #7 Differentiation, #15 Moat | 18% |
| Business Model | #8 Model Clarity, #9 Unit Economics | 12% |
| Execution | #10 Founder-Market Fit, #11 Tech Feasibility, #12 Regulatory | 16% |
| Scale & Distribution | #13 Scalability, #14 Distribution | 8% |

**Calculation:** Score each criterion 1-10, multiply by weight x 10, sum all = final score out of 100.

## 7. Viability Score System

Final viability scores map to historical success rates and recommended actions.

| Score Range | Rating | Est. Success Rate* | Recommendation |
|-------------|--------|---------------------|----------------|
| 85-100 | Exceptional | ~89% | Proceed with confidence. Invest aggressively. Raise funding if appropriate. |
| 75-84 | Strong | ~75% | Proceed, but address 1-2 minor concerns first. Strong foundation. |
| 65-74 | Moderate | ~52% | Proceed with caution. Address key challenges before scaling. Consider pivots. |
| 50-64 | Weak | ~28% | Significant pivots needed. Re-validate core assumptions. Do not invest heavily. |
| 0-49 | Very Weak | ~8% | Not recommended. Fundamental issues with market, model, or timing. Start over or abandon. |

*Success rates are modeled estimates from IdeaProof's dataset of 10,000+ validated ideas with 365+ follow-up studies. They are directional, not independently verified. Actual outcomes depend on execution, timing, and factors outside any scoring model.

### Score Interpretation Guidelines

- A score of 85+ does not guarantee success; it means the idea has strong fundamentals across all measured dimensions.
- A score below 50 does not mean the founder should quit; it means the current formulation needs major changes.
- The most impactful lever is **Market Timing (35%)**. A perfectly built product in a market that isn't ready will fail.
- Scores should be recalculated after each validation step as new evidence emerges.

## 8. Industry-Specific Benchmarks

Use these benchmarks to contextualize validation results. A 3% conversion rate means different things in SaaS vs mobile apps.

| Industry | Success Rate | Avg Viability Score | Time to PMF | Typical Launch Cost | Global TAM | CAC Range | LTV Range |
|----------|-------------|--------------------|--------------|--------------------|------------|-----------|-----------|
| SaaS B2B | 62% | 78/100 | 10-14 months | $50K-150K | $400B+ | $200-500 | $1,500-5,000 |
| E-commerce | 48% | 72/100 | 6-12 months | $20K-80K | $5.7T | $30-100 | $150-500 |
| Mobile App | 35% | 65/100 | 8-16 months | $30K-100K | $407B | $2-10 | $10-50 |
| FinTech | 58% | 75/100 | 12-18 months | $100K-500K | $310B | $50-200 | $500-2,000 |
| HealthTech | 55% | 74/100 | 14-24 months | $150K-1M | $280B | $100-400 | $1,000-5,000 |
| EdTech | 52% | 73/100 | 10-16 months | $40K-200K | $227B | $50-150 | $300-1,500 |
| Marketplace | 42% | 68/100 | 12-24 months | $60K-300K | $3.2T | $20-80 | $200-800 |
| FoodTech | 38% | 64/100 | 8-14 months | $50K-250K | $250B | $15-50 | $100-400 |

### How to Use These Benchmarks

- **LTV/CAC Ratio:** Minimum viable is 3:1. Below that, growth is unsustainable.
- **Time to PMF:** If you haven't found PMF within 1.5x the industry average, consider a major pivot.
- **Launch Cost:** Plan for 1.5-2x the high end for conservative budgeting.
- **Success Rate:** These are averages. Strong founders with validated ideas outperform; unvalidated ideas underperform.

### Industry-Specific Validation Adjustments

| Industry | Extra Validation Steps |
|----------|----------------------|
| SaaS B2B | Validate with decision-makers (not end users). Get LOIs. Pilot with 3-5 companies. |
| E-commerce | Test with paid ads before building. Validate unit economics including shipping/returns. |
| Mobile App | Prototype with Figma. Test retention before growth. App store optimization research. |
| FinTech | Regulatory landscape mapping mandatory. Compliance cost estimation. Trust/security validation. |
| HealthTech | Clinical validation pathway. HIPAA/regulatory requirements. Payer willingness to reimburse. |
| EdTech | Validate with both learners AND administrators/buyers. Efficacy measurement plan. |
| Marketplace | Solve chicken-and-egg: validate supply side first. Test both sides independently. |
| FoodTech | Unit economics with logistics. Cold chain/shelf life considerations. Local market testing. |

## 10. 5 Validation Mistakes

The most common errors founders make during validation, and how to avoid them.

### Mistake 1: Asking Friends and Family

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **The Problem** | Friends and family will validate your idea to protect your feelings. They are not your customers. |
| **The Data** | Studies show 90%+ of friends/family give positive feedback regardless of idea quality. |
| **The Fix** | Only count validation from strangers or loose acquaintances who match your target customer profile. |
| **Exception** | If your friends/family ARE your target customer and they commit money, that counts. |

### Mistake 2: Validating the Solution, Not the Problem

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **The Problem** | You fall in love with your solution and look for evidence it's brilliant, instead of checking whether the problem is real. |
| **The Data** | 42% of startups fail because no market need exists, the single largest failure mode. |
| **The Fix** | Spend Weeks 1-3 exclusively on problem validation. Do not mention your solution in interviews. |
| **Test** | If you remove your specific solution, does the problem still need solving? If yes, problem is validated. |

### Mistake 3: Treating Opinions as Validation

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **The Problem** | Survey responses, social media likes, and "I'd totally use that!" comments feel like validation but predict nothing. |
| **The Data** | Opinions (Level 5 in Validation Hierarchy) are 5x less predictive than transactions (Level 1). |
| **The Fix** | Always push for commitment. "Would you use this?" becomes "Would you pay $X today?" becomes "Here's the invoice." |
| **Rule of Thumb** | If no money or meaningful time commitment has changed hands, you have not validated anything. |

### Mistake 4: Skipping Market Size Analysis

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **The Problem** | You validate a real pain point but the market is too small to sustain a business. |
| **The Data** | TAM under $10M makes it nearly impossible to build a venture-scale business. Even bootstrapped businesses struggle below $5M TAM. |
| **The Fix** | Always calculate TAM/SAM/SOM in Week 1-2. Use both top-down and bottom-up methods. |
| **Trap** | "Everyone has this problem" is not market sizing. How many people will pay, and how much? |

### Mistake 5: Building Before Validating

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **The Problem** | Spending months building an MVP before talking to a single potential customer. |
| **The Data** | Validated startups reach PMF in ~8 months. Unvalidated startups typically never reach it. |
| **The Fix** | Follow the Master Framework sequentially. Do not write code until after Step 4 (pre-sales). |
| **Minimum** | At absolute minimum, complete 30 customer discovery interviews before building anything. |

## 11. Comparison Frameworks

Decision frameworks for common strategic choices. Use these when evaluating which path a startup should take.

### Bootstrap vs Venture Capital

| Dimension | Bootstrap | Venture Capital |
|-----------|-----------|-----------------|
| **Best When** | Profitable from day 1 possible, niche market, founder wants control | Large TAM ($1B+), winner-take-all market, network effects |
| **Capital Required** | $0-50K personal funds | $500K-5M+ (seed through Series A) |
| **Growth Expectation** | 20-50% YoY is great | 3-10x YoY expected |
| **Time to Profitability** | 6-18 months (must be fast) | 3-7 years (growth > profit) |
| **Founder Control** | 100% retained | Diluted 20-50%+ over rounds |
| **Risk Profile** | Lower financial risk, slower growth | Higher stakes, faster growth potential |
| **Exit Expectation** | Optional (lifestyle or acquisition) | Required (IPO or large acquisition) |
| **Decision Criteria** | Choose if: TAM $10M-100M, clear path to revenue, want lifestyle flexibility | Choose if: TAM $1B+, winner-take-all dynamics, willing to go all-in |

### Angel vs Venture Capital

| Dimension | Angel Investors | Venture Capital |
|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Check Size** | $25K-500K | $500K-50M+ |
| **Stage** | Pre-seed, seed | Seed through IPO |
| **Due Diligence** | Light (days to weeks) | Heavy (weeks to months) |
| **Decision Maker** | Individual | Partnership (committee vote) |
| **Board Seat** | Rarely | Almost always (Series A+) |
| **Value Add** | Mentorship, introductions, domain expertise | Recruiting, follow-on funding, operational support |
| **Expectations** | 5-10x return, flexible timeline | 10-100x return, 7-10 year fund cycle |
| **Decision Criteria** | Choose if: early stage, need speed, want mentors | Choose if: proven traction, need large capital, ready for governance |

### Lean Startup vs Traditional

| Dimension | Lean Startup | Traditional |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|
| **Planning** | Hypotheses and experiments | Business plan and projections |
| **Product Development** | MVP, iterate based on data | Full product, then launch |
| **Customer Input** | Continuous from day 1 | Post-launch feedback |
| **Pivot Frequency** | Every 2-6 weeks if needed | Rare, costly |
| **Time to Market** | 4-12 weeks for first version | 6-18 months |
| **Risk Management** | Fail fast, fail cheap | Fail expensive, fail late |
| **Best For** | New markets, uncertain demand, software | Established markets, regulated industries, hardware |
| **Decision Criteria** | Choose if: high uncertainty, software product, small team | Choose if: known market, regulatory requirements, physical product |

### Product-Led Growth vs Sales-Led Growth

| Dimension | Product-Led (PLG) | Sales-Led |
|-----------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Acquisition** | Self-serve signup, freemium/trial | Outbound sales, demos, proposals |
| **ACV (Annual Contract Value)** | $0-5K typical | $10K-500K+ typical |
| **Sales Cycle** | Minutes to days | Weeks to months |
| **CAC** | Low ($10-100) | High ($500-5,000+) |
| **Scaling Mechanism** | Viral loops, word of mouth | Hire more sales reps |
| **Time to Revenue** | Fast (days) | Slow (months) |
| **Examples** | Slack, Notion, Figma, Dropbox | Salesforce, Palantir, Workday |
| **Decision Criteria** | Choose if: end-user is buyer, product is simple to try, low ACV | Choose if: complex product, enterprise buyers, high ACV, customization needed |

### Freemium vs Free Trial

| Dimension | Freemium | Free Trial |
|-----------|----------|------------|
| **Access** | Limited features forever | Full features for limited time |
| **Conversion Rate** | 2-5% to paid | 10-25% to paid |
| **User Volume** | High (large free tier) | Lower (time pressure filters) |
| **Revenue Delay** | Months to years | Days to weeks |
| **Best For** | Network-effect products, viral growth | Complex products where full value needs time to appear |
| **Risk** | Free tier too good, no upgrade incentive | Trial too short, users don't reach "aha" moment |
| **Decision Criteria** | Choose if: product improves with more users, clear free/paid boundary | Choose if: product value is realized quickly, clear upgrade triggers |

### B2B SaaS vs B2C SaaS

| Dimension | B2B SaaS | B2C SaaS |
|-----------|----------|----------|
| **Price Point** | $50-50,000+/month | $5-50/month |
| **Sales Cycle** | 2 weeks to 12 months | Instant to 7 days |
| **Churn Rate** | 3-7% annual (healthy) | 3-7% monthly (healthy) |
| **CAC** | $200-5,000 | $2-100 |
| **LTV** | $1,500-100,000+ | $50-500 |
| **Support Expectations** | High-touch, SLAs | Self-serve, community |
| **Distribution** | Sales team, partnerships, content | App stores, social, SEO, paid ads |
| **Decision Criteria** | Choose if: solving business pain, complex workflows, high switching cost | Choose if: mass market, simple use case, viral potential |

### No-Code vs Custom Development

| Dimension | No-Code / Low-Code | Custom Development |
|-----------|--------------------|--------------------|
| **Time to MVP** | Days to weeks | Weeks to months |
| **Cost to MVP** | $0-5K | $20K-200K+ |
| **Customization** | Limited to platform capabilities | Unlimited |
| **Scalability** | 100s to low 1,000s of users | Millions+ |
| **Technical Debt** | Low (platform manages it) | Accumulates, must manage |
| **Vendor Lock-in** | High | None (you own the code) |
| **Best For** | Validation, internal tools, simple products | Core product, complex logic, scale |
| **Decision Criteria** | Choose if: validating idea, non-technical founder, simple CRUD app | Choose if: validated idea, need performance/scale, complex business logic |

### Solo Founder vs Co-Founder

| Dimension | Solo Founder | Co-Founder(s) |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|
| **Decision Speed** | Fastest (no consensus needed) | Slower (alignment required) |
| **Skill Coverage** | Limited to one person | Complementary skills |
| **Fundraising** | Harder (VCs prefer teams) | Easier (de-risks execution) |
| **Emotional Load** | All on one person | Shared burden |
| **Equity** | 100% retained | Split (typically 50/50 or 60/40) |
| **Conflict Risk** | None | Co-founder breakups kill ~23% of startups |
| **Best For** | Bootstrapped, niche, solo-operator businesses | Venture-backed, complex products, rapid scaling |
| **Decision Criteria** | Choose if: full-stack capable, bootstrapping, want control | Choose if: need complementary skills, raising VC, building complex product |

## 12. Customer Discovery

### 50 Questions Across 10 Categories

#### Category 1: Problem Awareness (5 questions)

1. "What's the biggest challenge you face with [area]?"
2. "How would you describe this problem to a colleague?"
3. "When did you first notice this was a problem?"
4. "Is this problem getting worse, better, or staying the same?"
5. "Where does this rank among your top 3 priorities right now?"

#### Category 2: Current Solutions (5 questions)

6. "What do you currently use to handle [problem]?"
7. "How did you find your current solution?"
8. "What do you like about your current approach?"
9. "What frustrates you most about your current approach?"
10. "How much do you pay for your current solution (money and time)?"

#### Category 3: Frequency & Severity (5 questions)

11. "How often do you encounter this problem? Daily, weekly, monthly?"
12. "Walk me through the last time this happened."
13. "How long does it take to deal with each occurrence?"
14. "What's the worst-case scenario when this goes wrong?"
15. "How many people on your team are affected by this?"

#### Category 4: Impact & Cost (5 questions)

16. "What does this problem cost you in dollars per month/year?"
17. "How much revenue do you estimate you lose because of this?"
18. "What could you accomplish if this problem was solved?"
19. "How does this problem affect your customers or end users?"
20. "What's the opportunity cost of not solving this?"

#### Category 5: Decision-Making (5 questions)

21. "Who would need to approve purchasing a solution like this?"
22. "What's the typical buying process for tools at your company?"
23. "What budget category would this fall under?"
24. "What would make you switch from your current solution?"
25. "What's a deal-breaker that would prevent you from adopting something new?"

#### Category 6: Willingness to Pay (5 questions)

26. "If a solution existed that solved [specific pain], what would it be worth to you?"
27. "What's the most you've paid for a tool in this category?"
28. "Would you pay $X/month for a solution that [specific outcome]?" (test multiple price points)
29. "Would you prefer to pay per use, monthly, or annually?"
30. "At what price would this be an impulse buy vs needing approval?"

#### Category 7: Feature Priority (5 questions)

31. "If you could only have one feature, what would it be?"
32. "What's the minimum a solution would need to do to be useful?"
33. "What features would make you say 'I need this now' vs 'nice to have'?"
34. "What integrations are essential for you?"
35. "How important is mobile access vs desktop?"

#### Category 8: Adoption & Switching (5 questions)

36. "What would the onboarding process need to look like?"
37. "How long are you willing to spend learning a new tool?"
38. "What would make your team resist adopting something new?"
39. "Have you tried switching tools before? What happened?"
40. "What would make you champion this tool internally?"

#### Category 9: Competition & Alternatives (5 questions)

41. "What other solutions have you evaluated recently?"
42. "Why did you choose your current solution over alternatives?"
43. "What's missing from every solution you've seen?"
44. "Who do you consider the leaders in this space?"
45. "If you were building a solution, what would you do differently?"

#### Category 10: Future & Vision (5 questions)

46. "How do you see this problem evolving in the next 2-3 years?"
47. "What trends are affecting your industry that relate to this?"
48. "If this problem were solved, what would you tackle next?"
49. "Who else should I talk to about this problem?"
50. "Can I follow up with you when we have something to show?"

### Interview Best Practices

| Practice | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| **Duration** | 20-30 minutes max. Respect their time. |
| **Recording** | Ask permission first. If no, take detailed notes immediately after. |
| **Compensation** | $25-50 gift card or offer to share findings. Not required for B2B. |
| **Setting** | Video call or in-person. Never email surveys for discovery. |
| **Warm-up** | Start with easy, open-ended questions. Save sensitive (pricing) questions for after rapport. |
| **Follow-up** | Send a thank-you within 24 hours. Ask for referrals. |
| **Synthesis** | After every 5-10 interviews, document patterns and update hypotheses. |

### Sample Sizes & Pattern Recognition

| Sample Size | What You Learn | Confidence Level |
|-------------|---------------|------------------|
| 5-10 interviews | Initial themes emerge, directional only | Low (exploratory) |
| 10-15 interviews | Patterns solidify, key pain points clear | Medium (patterns visible) |
| 15-25 interviews | Diminishing new insights, strong signal | High (convergence) |
| 30-50 interviews | Statistical significance, segment differences appear | Very high (validation-grade) |
| 50+ interviews | Quantitative confidence, sub-segment analysis | Research-grade |

**Rule of thumb:** If you hear the same unprompted pain point from 15+ people across 30-50 interviews, the problem is validated. If every interview surfaces a different problem, there is no convergent pain to solve.

## 13. Product-Market Fit Indicators

15 signals that indicate PMF has been achieved (or is close). No single signal is sufficient. Look for 5+ signals co-occurring.

| # | Signal | Threshold | How to Measure |
|---|--------|-----------|----------------|
| 1 | **Sean Ellis Test** | 40%+ say "very disappointed" if product disappeared | Survey: "How would you feel if you could no longer use [product]?" Options: Very disappointed, Somewhat disappointed, Not disappointed, N/A |
| 2 | **Organic > Paid Acquisition** | 50%+ of new users from word of mouth | Attribution tracking, "How did you hear about us?" |
| 3 | **Retention Curve Flattening** | 20-40%+ retained at 3 months (varies by industry) | Cohort analysis, plot retention curves by weekly/monthly cohort |
| 4 | **NPS Score** | 50+ (world-class is 70+) | NPS survey: "How likely are you to recommend [product] to a friend?" (0-10 scale) |
| 5 | **Organic Growth Rate** | 30%+ month-over-month in early stage | Track new signups from non-paid channels |
| 6 | **Low Voluntary Churn** | <5% monthly for B2C, <2% monthly for B2B | (Customers lost in period) / (Customers at start of period) |
| 7 | **Expansion Revenue** | Existing customers increase spend over time | Net revenue retention > 100% (ideally 110-130%) |
| 8 | **Inbound Interest** | Prospects contact you without outbound effort | Track inbound demo requests, contact form submissions |
| 9 | **Usage Depth** | Users engage with multiple features, not just one | Feature adoption rates, sessions per user, time in product |
| 10 | **User-Generated Content** | Users create templates, guides, or workflows for others | Community activity, user guides, Stack Overflow questions |
| 11 | **Competitive Switching** | Users switch from established competitors to you | Track "switched from" data in onboarding surveys |
| 12 | **Pricing Power** | Users accept price increases without significant churn | A/B test pricing, monitor churn after increases |
| 13 | **Time to Value** | New users reach "aha moment" quickly | Track time from signup to first key action (should be < 1 day) |
| 14 | **Support Ticket Quality** | Users ask "how do I do more?" not "this is broken" | Categorize support tickets: feature requests vs bugs vs complaints |
| 15 | **Team Morale Signal** | Support and sales teams report enthusiastic customers | Qualitative, but real. Happy customers make happy teams. |

### PMF Scoring

Count how many of the 15 signals you've achieved:

| Signals Achieved | PMF Status |
|-----------------|------------|
| 0-3 | **Pre-PMF.** Keep iterating. Do not scale. |
| 4-6 | **Approaching PMF.** Double down on what's working. |
| 7-10 | **PMF Achieved.** Begin scaling acquisition. |
| 11-15 | **Strong PMF.** Scale aggressively. Defend moat. |

### The PMF Spectrum (Not Binary)

PMF is not a single moment. It exists on a spectrum:

```
No fit -----> Emerging fit -----> PMF -----> Strong PMF -----> Dominant PMF
(pivot)       (iterate)          (scale)    (expand)          (defend)
```

- **No fit:** Nobody cares. Time to pivot.
- **Emerging fit:** Some users love it, most don't. Find what the lovers have in common.
- **PMF:** A defined segment loves it and retains. Scale acquisition for that segment.
- **Strong PMF:** Multiple segments retain. Expand to adjacent segments.
- **Dominant PMF:** Market leader in your category. Focus on moat and expansion.

### Sequoia Arc PMF Framework (3 Archetypes)

Different types of PMF require different strategies:

| Archetype | Description | Strategy | Example |
|-----------|-------------|----------|---------|
| **Hair on Fire** | Customer has urgent, burning problem | Be the parachute rescue. Speed matters most. | Security breach response tools |
| **Hard Fact** | Better solution to acknowledged problem | Demonstrably outperform incumbents on key dimension. | Square (better POS than existing solutions) |
| **Future Vision** | Customer doesn't know they need it yet | Build conviction, amass headstart. Requires patience and education. | Early iPhone, early Airbnb |

Identify which archetype fits your idea - it determines your GTM approach and how you measure PMF.

## Quick Reference: Minimum Viable Validation Checklist

Before writing any code or spending significant money, confirm:

- [ ] 30+ customer discovery interviews completed
- [ ] 15+ people describe the same pain unprompted
- [ ] TAM calculated at >= $10M (both top-down and bottom-up)
- [ ] Competitor landscape mapped (at least 5 alternatives analyzed)
- [ ] Landing page tested with 500+ visitors, 2%+ conversion
- [ ] 3+ prospects willing to pay or sign LOI before product exists
- [ ] Unit economics modeled with LTV/CAC >= 3:1
- [ ] One clear differentiator identified that competitors can't easily copy

If you can't check all 8 boxes, you have not validated. Go back to the step you're missing.
