# Decision-Making & Strategic Thinking Frameworks

A reference guide for the mental models and decision frameworks every CEO should have in their toolkit.

---

## 1. Decision Classification: Bezos Type 1 / Type 2

**Type 1 (One-Way Door):** Irreversible or nearly irreversible. Think hard, consult widely, take your time.

- Examples: choosing a co-founder, selling the company, core technical architecture, entering a regulated market

**Type 2 (Two-Way Door):** Reversible. Move fast, course-correct later.

- Examples: pricing page A/B test, trying a new marketing channel, changing an internal workflow, hiring a contractor for a pilot

**The 70% Rule:** Most decisions should be made with roughly 70% of the information you wish you had. Waiting for 90%+ means you're moving too slowly.

**The Growth Trap:** As organizations grow, they instinctively apply Type 1 process (committees, review cycles, sign-offs) to every decision, including Type 2 ones. This kills speed. The job of leadership is to protect Type 2 velocity by explicitly classifying decisions and pushing authority downward.

---

## 2. Disagree and Commit

After a decision is made, everyone commits 100%, even those who disagreed.

- **Bezos example:** He personally disagreed with greenlighting a particular Amazon Studios show, but told the team: "I disagree, but I'm committing to you. Let's make it the best version possible."
- **Pairs with Type 1/2:** If the decision is reversible (Type 2), commit fast. You'll learn more from doing than from debating.
- **Why it matters:** Without this norm, organizations either deadlock on disagreements or build resentment. Disagree-and-commit gives people a legitimate channel to voice concerns AND a clear protocol for moving forward.

## 3. RAPID Framework (Bain & Company)

A decision-role framework for when decisions stall because nobody owns them (common once you're past 10-15 people).

| Role | Meaning | Responsibility |
|------|---------|----------------|
| **R** | Recommend | Gathers data, proposes options, builds the case |
| **A** | Agree | Must sign off (has veto power, used sparingly) |
| **P** | Perform | Executes after the decision is made |
| **I** | Input | Consulted for expertise, but doesn't decide |
| **D** | Decide | The single person who makes the call |

**Key principle:** The "D" should sit as close to implementation as possible. If the CEO is the "D" on everything, the organization bottlenecks.

**When to use:** Any recurring decision type that keeps getting stuck, cross-functional initiatives, decisions that involve multiple stakeholders with unclear authority.

## 4. Pre-Mortem Analysis (Gary Klein)

Instead of a post-mortem (after failure), run a pre-mortem (before launch).

**How to run it:**

1. Gather the team. State: "Assume this project has already failed. It's 6 months from now and it went badly."
2. Each person writes plausible failure reasons silently (3 minutes).
3. Round-robin sharing, no discussion during sharing.
4. Group and prioritize the failure modes.
5. Build specific mitigations for the top risks.

**Evidence:** Research shows pre-mortems increase the ability to identify reasons for future outcomes by 30%.

**When to use:** Before any Type 1 decision, before major product launches, before raising a round, before hiring a key executive, before entering a new market.

## 5. Inversion Thinking (Charlie Munger)

Instead of asking "How do I succeed?", ask "How would I guarantee failure?" Then avoid those things.

**The process:**

1. Define the goal.
2. Invert: "What actions would guarantee we fail at this?"
3. List every way to fail.
4. Build your plan to systematically avoid each failure mode.

**Example:**
- Goal: Find product-market fit
- Inversion: "What guarantees we never find PMF?"
  - Never talk to users
  - Build in isolation for 12 months
  - Ignore churn data
  - Chase every feature request equally
  - Hire a big team before validating demand
- Plan: Do the opposite of each item above.

**Why it works:** Avoiding stupidity is often easier and more reliable than seeking brilliance. The path to success is often clearer when you map out the path to failure first.

## 6. Second-Order Thinking (Howard Marks)

First-order thinking: "This will happen."
Second-order thinking: "This will happen, and then what?"

**The exercise:** For any major decision, fill in three columns across three time horizons:

| Time Horizon | 1st Order Effect | 2nd Order Effect | 3rd Order Effect |
|-------------|------------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| 10 days | | | |
| 10 months | | | |
| 10 years | | | |

**Key insight:** Many extraordinary outcomes come from decisions that are first-order negative but second-order positive.

- Raising prices (1st: some customers leave) -> (2nd: remaining customers are higher quality, margins improve) -> (3rd: you can invest more in product, compounding advantage)
- Saying no to a big customer's custom request (1st: lose revenue) -> (2nd: keep product focused) -> (3rd: attract more ideal customers, faster growth)

**Rule of thumb:** If everyone agrees with your first-order analysis, you probably haven't thought deeply enough. The edge comes from second and third-order thinking.

## 7. Working Backwards / PR-FAQ (Amazon)

Write a mock press release announcing your finished product BEFORE you build anything.

**Press Release Structure (< 1 page):**

1. **Headline:** Name the product in a way the customer would understand
2. **Subheadline:** One sentence describing who it's for and what benefit they get
3. **Customer problem:** 1-2 paragraphs on the pain point
4. **Solution:** How the product solves it
5. **Customer quote:** A made-up quote from a happy customer
6. **How it works:** Simple explanation
7. **CTA:** "Get started at..."

**FAQ Structure (< 5 pages):**

- **External FAQ:** Questions a customer would ask (pricing, availability, how it differs from alternatives)
- **Internal FAQ:** Questions the team/leadership would ask (feasibility, cost, timeline, risks, dependencies)

**Review process:** Silent reading of the document, then discussion. No pre-reading, no PowerPoint. Forces clarity of thought.

**Why it works:** Forces you to articulate customer value before investing engineering time. If you can't write a compelling press release, the product probably isn't worth building.

## 8. Wartime vs Peacetime CEO (Ben Horowitz)

**Peacetime conditions:** Market is growing, you have a clear competitive advantage, things are generally working.

| Peacetime CEO | Wartime CEO |
|--------------|-------------|
| Empowers teams to make decisions | Micromanages survival-critical details |
| Builds broad-based consensus | Makes unilateral calls when needed |
| Defines and reinforces culture deliberately | Lets the war define the culture |
| Follows established protocols and processes | Violates protocol if it helps win |
| Encourages creativity and exploration | Demands focus and execution |
| Expands the mission | Narrows the mission to what matters now |

**Wartime conditions:** Existential threat, running out of runway, market collapse, critical competitor move, key team departure.

**The startup reality:** Most startups are wartime by default. You're fighting for survival, you haven't found PMF yet, runway is finite. The mistake is running peacetime playbooks (consensus-driven, process-heavy, exploration-focused) during wartime.

**Transition awareness:** The same company can shift between wartime and peacetime. Great CEOs recognize the shift and adapt their operating style accordingly.

## 9. Pivot vs Persevere Criteria

### Signals to PIVOT

- Engagement is flat after multiple iterations
- Customer acquisition cost (CAC) is high with no clear path to bringing it down
- User feedback is consistently misaligned with your value proposition
- 100+ customer conversations haven't revealed real demand
- The team is demoralized and losing conviction
- You're pivoting your messaging constantly to find resonance

### Signals to PERSEVERE

- Organic growth, even if small
- Key metrics are improving directionally (week-over-week, month-over-month)
- Sean Ellis test: >40% of users say they'd be "very disappointed" without the product
- Team is aligned and energized
- You're getting unsolicited inbound interest
- A small group of users absolutely loves the product

### Dalton Caldwell's "Warmth Test"

Good pivots get warmer toward your team's expertise, domain knowledge, and unfair advantages. Bad pivots get colder, moving further from what you know and who you know.

**Framework:** Before pivoting, ask: "Are we moving toward heat (our strengths, our network, our insight) or away from it?"

## 10. Prioritization Frameworks Compared

| Framework | Formula / Method | Best For | Speed |
|-----------|-----------------|----------|-------|
| **ICE** | Impact x Confidence x Ease (each 1-10) | Early-stage, weekly experiments | Fast (minutes) |
| **RICE** | (Reach x Impact x Confidence) / Effort | Quarterly roadmap prioritization | Moderate (hours) |
| **Weighted Scoring** | Custom criteria with custom weights | High-stakes, multi-month decisions | Slow (half-day workshop) |
| **Eisenhower Matrix** | Urgent/Important 2x2 grid | Personal time management | Instant |
| **MoSCoW** | Must / Should / Could / Won't | Requirements triage | Fast |
| **WSJF** | Weighted Shortest Job First | Scaled Agile, large orgs | Moderate |

### Suggested Progression by Stage

- **Pre-seed:** MoSCoW (simple, fast, good enough)
- **Seed / Series A:** RICE (adds rigor without overhead)
- **Series B+:** WSJF or Weighted Scoring (handles complexity at scale)

**Anti-pattern:** Using a heavyweight framework too early. If you're pre-PMF, you don't need a half-day prioritization workshop. ICE scores on a whiteboard are fine.

## 11. Task-Relevant Maturity (Andy Grove, "High Output Management")

How closely you monitor someone should depend on their maturity **for that specific task**, not their general competence or seniority.

| TRM Level | Management Style | Check-in Frequency |
|-----------|-----------------|-------------------|
| **Low** | Structured guidance, detailed instructions, frequent check-ins | Daily or every other day |
| **Medium** | Mutual discussion, shared problem-solving | Weekly |
| **High** | Set goals and step back, minimal oversight | Bi-weekly or as needed |

**Critical distinction:** TRM is per-task, not per-person. A brilliant engineer who's never done sales has low TRM for sales. A senior marketer taking on their first management role has low TRM for people management.

**Implication:** Treating everyone the same (either all hands-off or all micromanaged) is a failure of leadership. The right level of involvement depends on context.

## 12. Regret Minimization Framework (Jeff Bezos)

Project yourself to age 80. Ask: "Will I regret NOT having done this?"

**The Bezos origin story:** Used this framework to decide whether to leave his job at D.E. Shaw to start Amazon. At 80, would he regret not trying? Yes. Would he regret trying and failing? No, because at least he'd know.

**When to use:** Career-defining or company-defining fork-in-the-road moments. Not for everyday decisions.

**The test:**
1. Imagine yourself at 80, looking back.
2. "Will I regret not having tried this?"
3. "Will I regret the path not taken more than the risk of failure?"

**Limitation:** This framework biases toward action, which is usually right for founders but isn't always the correct call. Pair it with pre-mortem analysis to make sure you're not walking into avoidable disaster.
